Clinical Psychology Board Examination

Diagnostic, Ethical, & Practical Case Scenarios

Part 1: Diagnostic Reasoning & Differential Diagnosis
Question 1

A 28-year-old patient presents with a 6-month history of social withdrawal, severe lack of motivation (avolition), and a flat affect. They deny hearing voices or feeling depressed, but their academic performance has completely collapsed. What is the primary differential to consider?

Expected Diagnosis: Schizophrenia (Prodromal or Negative Symptoms phase) vs. Major Depressive Disorder.

Clinical Focus: Distinguishing between the negative symptoms of schizophrenia and the vegetative symptoms of severe depression.

Question 2

A client describes periods of intense creative energy, sleeping only 3 hours a night for a week, and spending their entire savings on art supplies. Immediately following this, they experienced a two-week crash of profound fatigue and dark thoughts. They have no prior psychiatric history. Which diagnosis is most strictly indicated?

Expected Diagnosis: Bipolar I Disorder (if the manic episode caused severe impairment/hospitalization) or Bipolar II (if the energetic period was hypomanic and followed by a major depressive episode).

Clinical Focus: Tracking the duration, severity, and sequence of mood shifts.

Question 3

A patient presents with extreme anxiety about their health. They are convinced they have a rare neurological disease despite completely normal neurological workups from three different specialists. They spend hours tracking minor bodily sensations. What is the most appropriate DSM-5-TR category?

Expected Diagnosis: Illness Anxiety Disorder (somatic symptoms are absent or minimal) vs. Somatic Symptom Disorder (if prominent, distressing physical symptoms are present).

Question 4

During an intake, a 35-year-old client reports frequent flashbacks, hypervigilance, and intense irritability. They state these symptoms started roughly three weeks after surviving a major multi-car highway accident. What is the provisional diagnosis?

Expected Diagnosis: Acute Stress Disorder (ASD).

Clinical Focus: Since the duration is under one month, it cannot yet be classified as Post-Traumatic Stress Disorder (PTSD).

Question 5

A patient exhibits a pervasive pattern of grandiosity, an intense need for admiration, and a total lack of empathy. However, during the session, when you gently challenge a statement they made, they fly into an intense, silent rage and later send an email threatening to report you to the licensing board. What personality profile does this illustrate?

Expected Diagnosis: Narcissistic Personality Disorder (specifically showing a "narcissistic injury" or narcissistic rage).

Question 6

A 19-year-old college student is brought in by their roommates. They have become intensely suspicious, believing the university professors are altering exam questions specifically to make them fail. They state they hear a faint whispering sound when the room is completely silent. The symptoms began two months ago. What is the provisional diagnosis?

Expected Diagnosis: Schizophreniform Disorder.

Clinical Focus: The duration is greater than 1 month but less than the 6 months required for a diagnosis of Schizophrenia.

Question 7

A client presents with severe panic attacks that seem to hit "out of the blue." As a result, they have stopped using public transit, going to crowded malls, or leaving their house alone for fear of having an attack where escape might be difficult. What dual presentation is this?

Expected Diagnosis: Panic Disorder with Agoraphobia.

Question 8

An individual presents with a history of unstable, intense relationships, chronic feelings of emptiness, recurrent self-harming behavior, and frantic efforts to avoid real or imagined abandonment. During sessions, they alternate between praising you as the "only therapist who ever cared" and accusing you of being cold and detached. What condition is this?

Expected Diagnosis: Borderline Personality Disorder (BPD), showcasing the defense mechanism of "splitting."

Question 9

A 45-year-old corporate executive reports that for the past two and a half years, they have felt consistently low, low-energy, had poor concentration, and struggled with insomnia. They note they haven't been without these symptoms for more than two months at a time, though they've never been completely incapacitated by them. What is the likely diagnosis?

Expected Diagnosis: Persistent Depressive Disorder (Dysthymia).

Question 10

A client presents with an extreme, irrational fear of being judged, humiliated, or scrutinized by others in public spaces, specifically when speaking, eating, or writing in front of people. They acknowledge the fear is excessive but completely avoid these situations. What is the diagnosis?

Expected Diagnosis: Social Anxiety Disorder (Social Phobia).

Question 11

A patient presents with recurrent, intrusive thoughts about hurting their child, which they find horrifying and completely contrary to their desires (ego-dystonic). To cope with these thoughts, they must tap the kitchen counter exactly twelve times every time the thought occurs. What is the diagnosis?

Expected Diagnosis: Obsessive-Compulsive Disorder (OCD).

Clinical Focus: Differentiating ego-dystonic obsessive thoughts from ego-syntonic psychotic impulses.

Question 12

A 50-year-old individual is brought to therapy by their spouse. The spouse reports that the patient has become increasingly rigid, perfectionistic, and obsessed with rules, schedules, and lists, to the point where they cannot finish projects because their standards are too high. The patient sees no issue with their behavior. What is the likely diagnosis?

Expected Diagnosis: Obsessive-Compulsive Personality Disorder (OCPD).

Clinical Focus: Recognizing the ego-syntonic nature of OCPD versus the ego-dystonic nature of OCD.

Question 13

A patient reports that they frequently feel completely detached from their own body, as if they are watching themselves from the outside or living in a dreamlike, artificial world. Physical and neurological exams are clear. They remain oriented to reality during these episodes. What is the condition?

Expected Diagnosis: Depersonalization/Derealization Disorder.

Question 14

A client presents with severe memory loss regarding a highly traumatic physical assault they experienced a month ago. Neurological scans show no organic brain damage or medical explanation for the memory gap. What is the likely diagnosis?

Expected Diagnosis: Dissociative Amnesia.

Question 15

A 24-year-old client describes a lifelong pattern of avoiding social interactions, not out of a desire to be alone, but due to severe feelings of inadequacy and an intense hypersensitivity to any potential negative criticism or rejection. What personality disorder fits this profile?

Expected Diagnosis: Avoidant Personality Disorder.

Part 2: Managing Difficult Patient Typologies
Question 16

How should a clinician respond when a patient with Borderline Personality Disorder engages in "splitting" by telling you that your co-therapist/colleague is "awful and incompetent" while you are "a savior"?

Clinical Guideline: Maintain a neutral stance, avoid validating the degradation of the colleague, gently point out the pattern of idealization and devaluation, and maintain clear, consistent communication with your team.

Question 17

What is the primary therapeutic risk when treating a passive-aggressive patient who consistently agrees to behavioral homework assignments in session but never completes them?

Clinical Guideline: The therapist may fall into a countertransference trap of becoming overly directive, demanding, or punitive, which reinforces the patient’s underlying belief that authority figures are controlling.

Question 18

A highly intellectualized patient spends the entire session analyzing the evolutionary psychology of their grief rather than actually feeling or expressing the sadness of their recent loss. What is the therapist’s best intervention?

Clinical Guideline: Gently interrupt the cognitive analysis and redirect focus to the immediate somatic experience in the room (e.g., "As you read those statistics, I notice your hands are clenched. What are you feeling physically right now?").

Question 19

When dealing with a patient with Histrionic Personality Disorder who uses exaggerated, highly emotional language but lacks specific depth or detail in their descriptions, how should the therapist structure interventions?

Clinical Guideline: Use highly structured, clarifying questions that require the patient to ground their sweeping statements in concrete, specific details and behavior.

Question 20

A patient with Paranoid Personality Disorder accuses you of taking notes in order to sell their personal information to data agencies. What is the counterproductive response?

Clinical Guideline: Arguing, excessively defending yourself, or trying to logically convince them they are wrong. Acknowledge their need for safety, offer to let them look at the notes, and maintain an open, non-defensive, transparent posture.

Question 21

How do you handle a "Help-Rejecting Complainer"—a patient who continually asks for advice or solutions but immediately shoots down every suggestion with "Yes, but..."?

Clinical Guideline: Shift away from offering concrete solutions entirely. Instead, validate the frustration of feeling stuck and explore the underlying function or comfort of remaining in the problem state.

Question 22

A patient diagnosed with Antisocial Personality Disorder attempts to flatter you by commenting on your appearance and offering to give you a discount at their luxury business. What clinical boundary must be enforced?

Clinical Guideline: Explicitly decline the offer, name the boundary violation neutrally without being punitive, and explore the patient's intent behind trying to alter the professional nature of the relationship.

Question 23

What is the optimal therapeutic stance when working with a highly dependent patient who refuses to make any life decisions (e.g., career moves, relationship choices) without your explicit permission or approval?

Clinical Guideline: Refuse to make decisions for them. Reflect the anxiety of autonomy back onto them, and validate their micro-choices to gradually build self-efficacy.

Question 24

A patient with severe Major Depressive Disorder presents with an completely flat affect, responding only in one-word answers ("IDK," "Yeah," "Fine"). How should you adjust your clinical pacing?

Clinical Guideline: Reduce the density of open-ended, complex questions. Use shorter, more direct check-ins, allow for long periods of comfortable silence, and avoid pressuring them to generate elaborate narratives.

Question 25

An anxious, obsessive patient demands that you give them a 100% guarantee that their upcoming flight will not crash before they agree to do an exposure exercise. How do you respond?

Clinical Guideline: Refuse to provide false reassurance. Instead, help them build tolerance for the inherent uncertainty of life, pointing out that seeking absolute certainty is the engine driving their anxiety.

Question 26

During a crisis session, an angry, dysregulated patient stands up, steps into your personal space, and begins shouting profanities at you. What is your immediate priority and action?

Clinical Guideline: Physical safety and de-escalation. Adopt a non-threatening posture, step back to increase physical space, use a calm, lowered voice tone, keep your hands visible, and ensure you have a clear path to the exit.

Question 27

A patient with Somatic Symptom Disorder becomes angry when you suggest incorporating psychological coping mechanisms for their chronic back pain, stating, "You think this is all in my head!" How do you realign the therapeutic alliance?

Clinical Guideline: Validate that their physical pain is 100% real and distressing. Explain that the brain and body are deeply connected, and that treating the psychological stress caused by chronic pain is standard practice to help lower the nervous system's pain signals.

Question 28

How do you manage a patient who consistently crosses boundaries by texting your professional line at 2:00 AM for non-emergency issues, despite prior agreements regarding contact hours?

Clinical Guideline: Do not reply to the late-night messages. Address the issue directly at the very beginning of the next session, restating the framework of the therapeutic contract and exploring what feelings came up for them that prompted the midnight texts.

Question 29

A client with a severe substance use disorder arrives at your office visibly intoxicated, slurring their words and smelling of alcohol. What is the appropriate protocol?

Clinical Guideline: Do not conduct the therapy session, as they cannot cognitively engage or give informed consent. Assess their physical safety, ensure they have a safe ride home (do not let them drive), and reschedule the appointment once they are sober.

Question 30

When treating a patient who has experienced severe developmental trauma, they begin to view you as an abusive parent figure during a moment of confrontation. What psychological phenomenon is occurring, and how do you handle it?

Clinical Guideline: A negative transference reaction / trauma reenactment. The therapist must remain calm, non-defensive, and highlight the contrast between the past abuser and the current supportive environment in the room.

Part 3: Ethics, Boundaries, and Crisis Management
Question 31

A patient mentions during a routine session that they sometimes feel so stressed they wish they could just sleep and never wake up. They deny having a plan, access to lethal means, or immediate intent. How do you categorize their suicidality?

Ethical Guideline: Passive Suicidal Ideation.

Clinical Step: Conduct a full, structured safety assessment, document the absence of an active plan/intent, and create a collaborative coping/crisis protocol.

Question 32

A client explicitly states that they are planning to severely assault their ex-partner tomorrow afternoon and have purchased a weapon to do so. Under what legal/ethical precedent must you break confidentiality?

Ethical Guideline: The Duty to Protect / Duty to Warn (commonly derived from the Tarasoff ruling). You must take reasonable steps to warn the intended victim and notify law enforcement.

Question 33

You realize that a new patient on your schedule is the direct supervisor of your close personal friend. What kind of ethical issue does this present, and what is the best course of action?

Ethical Guideline: A potential or actual Dual Relationship / Conflict of Interest. If it compromises clinical objectivity or confidentiality, you should refer the patient to another qualified provider.

Question 34

A patient brings you an expensive, hand-carved watch worth $500 at the end of a successful treatment cycle to show their gratitude. What ethical considerations must you evaluate before accepting or refusing?

Ethical Guideline: The monetary value, the therapeutic intent, the patient's cultural background, the timing of the gift, and whether accepting it changes the professional boundaries of the relationship. (A gift of this value should generally be declined or redirected to a clinical setting donation).

Question 35

During a session with an elderly patient, you notice suspicious, unexplained bruising on their forearms and they accidentally slip up, mentioning that their adult caregiver frequently locks them in their room when they get "annoying." What is your legal obligation?

Ethical Guideline: You are a mandated reporter for elder abuse. You must immediately file a report with Adult Protective Services (APS) or the appropriate regulatory agency.

Question 36

A 15-year-old minor client tells you they smoke marijuana on the weekends. Their parents demand to see your complete session notes to find out if their child is doing drugs. How do you navigate confidentiality?

Ethical Guideline: Balance the minor's right to privacy (essential for maintaining the therapeutic alliance) with the parents' legal rights. Ideally, discuss with the minor what information can be shared safely, or hold a mediated session, while withholding raw notes if allowed by local laws to protect the therapeutic space.

Question 37

You realize you are feeling intense romantic or sexual attraction toward a long-term client. What must you immediately do?

Ethical Guideline: Seek immediate clinical supervision or consultation to manage countertransference. Do not act on these feelings under any circumstances, as it is a severe ethical violation. If objectivity cannot be restored, terminate the relationship and refer out safely.

Question 38

A patient drops out of therapy abruptly. Three months later, they contact you asking to grab a casual coffee as friends since they are no longer your patient. What do ethical codes dictate?

Ethical Guideline: Professional associations (like the APA or ACA) prohibit transitioning from a client to a personal friend due to the permanent power imbalance created during therapy. You must decline the request.

Question 39

Under what specific conditions can a clinician initiate an involuntary psychiatric hold (e.g., 5150 / Baker Act) for a psychiatric patient?

Ethical Guideline: When, due to a suspected mental health disorder, the person is determined to be an imminent danger to themselves, an imminent danger to others, or gravely disabled (unable to provide for their basic food, clothing, or shelter).

Question 40

A client tells you they were physically abused by a prominent local therapist who practiced in your city five years ago. What is your ethical obligation regarding reporting the colleague?

Ethical Guideline: Maintain your client's confidentiality above all else. You cannot report the other therapist without the client's explicit, written consent. Your focus must remain on treating the client's trauma.

Question 41

A patient reveals they are embezzling millions of dollars from a massive tech corporation. They have not hurt anyone physically. Can you break confidentiality to report this corporate crime?

Ethical Guideline: No. Financial crimes do not meet the threshold of imminent risk of physical harm or death required to breach a patient's confidentiality without a subpoena.

Question 42

You are using a new, highly experimental somatic therapy technique that you read about in an unvetted online forum. What ethical principle must you practice before using it on a vulnerable patient?

Ethical Guideline: Informed Consent and Practice within Competence. You must explicitly inform the patient of the experimental nature, risks, and lack of empirical backing, and ensure you aren't using them as an untrained trial.

Question 43

A patient who has been struggling with chronic suicidal ideation suddenly shows up to a session looking completely calm, serene, and unburdened. They state everything is "finally sorted out" and give you their favorite watch as a souvenir. What should this change in presentation alert you to?

Ethical Guideline: An immediate, high-risk spike in active suicide potential. The sudden calm often indicates the patient has resolved their ambivalence by finalizing a concrete plan to end their life.

Question 44

You receive a subpoena from an attorney demanding the medical records of a client involved in a personal injury lawsuit. What is your first step?

Ethical Guideline: Contact the client to determine if they wish to waive or assert their privilege. Consult your professional liability insurance or legal counsel before releasing any documents to ensure you do not inadvertently violate privacy laws.

Question 45

What is the ethical implication of continuing to provide and bill for psychotherapy sessions with a patient who has made no measurable clinical progress over two years and is simply using the time to chat about daily trivia?

Ethical Guideline: It violates the principle of Beneficence / avoiding exploitation. If a patient is no longer deriving therapeutic benefit from treatment, the therapist must discuss termination and offer referrals.

Part 4: Theoretical Modalities & Technical Interventions
Question 46

A Cognitive Behavioral Therapist (CBT) asks a client to track their thoughts using an ABC worksheet when they experience a sudden drop in mood. What does ABC stand for in this clinical context?

Theoretical Verification: Activating Event, Beliefs (automatic thoughts), and Consequences (emotional or behavioral outcomes).

Question 47

In Psychodynamic therapy, when a patient forgets their appointment time or suddenly switches topics the moment a painful memory about their mother is brought up, what clinical concept is being observed?

Theoretical Verification: Resistance.

Question 48

An Acceptance and Commitment Therapy (ACT) clinician helps a patient see their anxious thoughts as merely passing words rather than absolute truths (e.g., changing "I am a failure" to "I am noticing the thought that I am a failure"). What is this core process called?

Theoretical Verification: Cognitive Defusion.

Question 49

During a Dialectical Behavior Therapy (DBT) session, the therapist balances the strategy of validating the patient’s current emotional pain exactly as it is, while simultaneously pushing for what counter-strategy?

Theoretical Verification: Behavioral Change (The fundamental dialectic of DBT is balancing Acceptance and Change).

Question 50

A client with a severe phobia of dogs is guided through a hierarchy of fears, starting from looking at a drawing of a dog, to standing near a real dog, while simultaneously practicing deep muscle relaxation. What behaviorist technique is this?

Theoretical Verification: Systematic Desensitization.
